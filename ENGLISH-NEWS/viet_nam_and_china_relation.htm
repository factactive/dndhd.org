Viet Nam and China warmly relationship today

By David Brunnstrom

HANOI (Reuters) - Vietnam's Communist Party chief Nong Duc Manh told China's parliamentary leader Li Peng on Saturday he expected friendly ties and cooperation between the communist neighbors would continue to improve. Meeting Li at the presidential palace in Hanoi, Manh hailed the achievements of China's Communist Party, especially the past 20 years of reform and opening to the outside world.

Manh said present-day Vietnam-China relations were based on "16 Golden Words," including "neighborliness, friendship, multifaceted cooperation and long-term stability." Manh, who embraced Li warmly, said his visit was just the latest high-ranking exchange between the countries.

"(It) will, I think, make an important contribution to creating a new momentum in friendly relations and in the multi-faceted cooperation between the two parties, states and peoples," he said. No details have merged of talks since Li arrived on Friday on what has been described as an "official friendship visit." But discussions were expected to include comparisons of respective political and economic conditions, programs of reform and territorial issues, including rivalry in the South China Sea.

On the business side, further talks are expected on cooperation in hydropower and mining. Analysts say Li, chairman of China's National People's Congress, would be keen to assess Vietnam's leadership changes since the five-yearly Communist Party congress on April, at which Manh was elected in place of Le Kha Phieu. Earlier in the day, Li was greeted with a hug from Phieu, a man whose downfall was blamed by some diplomats and party sources on a perceived excessive closeness to Beijing.

RIVALS DESPITE SHARED IDEOLOGY

Among other things, Phieu has been accused of giving too much away in border negotiations with China, a traditional rival of Vietnam despite shared ideology.Phieu told Li Vietnam and China were proud of shared socialist ideals, "even though each country's path has specific features." Li will stay in Vietnam until Monday. He comes at a time when Hanoi is working to finalize amendments to its 1992 constitution, which are expected to enshrine rights of the private sector.

Last month, a government spokeswoman said Vietnam was closely watching China's plans to allow entrepreneurs to join the Communist Party, but added that each ruling party needed to take into account its own specific conditions. Ahead of its April congress, Vietnam's Communist Party rejected a proposal to allow members to engage in business.In a meeting with President Tran Duc Luong, Li conveyed an invitation from China's President Jiang Zemin for him to visit Beijing, a city he visited late last year.

The official Vietnam News Agency quoted Li as saying China was looking forward to a visit to Beijing by Manh, but gave no dates for this visit. It said Li's counterpart, National Assembly chairman Nguyen Van An, had accepted a similar invitation.

Li met Phieu's predecessor Do Muoi on Friday afternoon. Visiting communist dignitaries have traditionally called on past party bosses, but Li's meeting with Phieu appears unusual given that Vietnam's leadership battle was reputedly extremely acrimonious.

Vietnam and China normalized diplomatic ties in 1991, having fought a brief but bloody war in 1979 after Vietnam invaded Cambodia and ousted the Beijing-backed Khmer Rouge. Though relations have since greatly improved, the countries remain at odds over disputed and reputedly oil-rich territory in the South China Sea, including the Spratly archipelago.
