Vietnam frees 10,000 to mark anniversary

By David Brunnstrom

HANOI, Aug 30 (Reuters) - Vietnam said on Wednesday it was freeing more than 10,000 prisoners in its second mass jail clear-out of the year, among them an elderly Vietnamese-Canadian whose daughter it executed for heroin trafficking this year.

Releasing septuagenarian Tran Thi Cam, whose 44-year-old daughter Nguyen Thi Hiep was shot by firing squad in April, should help patch relations with Ottawa, which was incensed by the execution and downgraded ties.

Cam was among 61 foreigners covered in the presidential amnesty for 10,693 convicts announced ahead of the communist ruled country's 55th independence day on Saturday.

Vice Minister for Public Security Le The Tiem said the foreigners came from countries including the United States, Canada, Australia, France, Pakistan, Thailand, Laos and Cambodia.

He said the amnesty took affect from August 29 and hoped all those freed would be be out of jail by Saturday.

In April, Vietnam freed 12,264 prisoners, including 29 foreigners, in its largest ever prison amnesty, which marked the 25th anniversary of the communist victory in the Vietnam War.

It was unclear if any of the 10,408 being freed from jail this time and the 285 being released from suspended sentences included any political or religious prisoners.

Asked about two dissidents cited by London-based human rights group Amnesty International -- Nguyen Dinh Huy, founder of an illegal opposition party jailed for 15 years in 1995, and Le Van Tinh, who has been serving a 20-year term -- Tiem said:

``They do not meet the conditions for the special amnesty at this time.''

Asked about two religious leaders of concern to the European Union -- Le Kim Bien and Phan Cong Hien of the Cao Dai Buddhist sect -- Nguyen Camh Dinh chairman of the president's office, said he could not remember all the names on the list.

Tiem said the amnesty covered prisoners who met stated criteria and ``really have repented and rehabilitated themselves.''

The prison board said earlier this week foreigners who had served a quarter of their sentences, or eight years of a life sentence, would be eligible for the amnesty.

Cam, in her mid 70s and jailed since 1996, fell into the category of ``other special cases,'' Dinh said.

Canada expressed outrage at Hiep's execution, saying Hanoi reneged onpromises to look at evidence Ottawa said cast doubt on her conviction. It suspended ministerial contacts and aid talks in retaliation.

On Monday, Canada strongly hinted it would normalise ties with Vietnam if Cam was freed. It said Vietnam had quietly transferred Hiep's remains to relatives on August 19.

A Canadian diplomat said earlier that four Canadians were detained in Vietnam and it was not clear if all would be freed.

Rights groups estimate Vietnam holds at least dozens of dissidents in jail and greatly restricts movement of dozens it has previously freed.

They say it is difficult to determine exact numbers of prisoners of conscience because of Vietnam's climate of secrecy.
